]]> http://www.decisionsigma.com/blog/accelerating-time-to-value-decisionsigma-pre-built-spend-analytics-raw-material-price-forecasting-should-cost-modelling/feed/ 0 Creating Should-Cost Model using Excel vs DecisionSigma Analytics http://www.decisionsigma.com/blog/creating-should-cost-model-using-excel-vs-creating-within-decisionsigma-analytics/ http://www.decisionsigma.com/blog/creating-should-cost-model-using-excel-vs-creating-within-decisionsigma-analytics/#respond Mon, 30 Oct 2017 02:23:23 +0000 http://www.decisionsigma.com/blog/?p=199 Continue reading Creating Should-Cost Model using Excel vs DecisionSigma Analytics]]> Should cost model (“clean sheet model”), originally developed and adopted by the Department of Defense to determine fair prices of equipment they procure, started getting popularity among industry in early 90’s. Now should cost modelling is one of most dominant techniques used by companies that has high direct costs.
Should-cost modeling requires organizations to think as if they are manufacturing the product that they are buying. This requires them to estimate materials cost, labor cost, overhead cost, and profit margin for unit quantity of the product they are procuring.
Generally, engineering team within the organization comes up with list of materials used and they quantity, manufacturing team estimates the labor and then procurement team estimates approximate material cost and assign a reasonable profit margin to determine should cost. Sometimes companies work with external agencies to put together the estimated data for part of their material.
Some organizations still think estimating the model is too complex and time consuming, But they fail to ignore that many times material cost constitutes major portion(sometimes >60%) of the cost and if they simply can estimate material cost then they covered 60% of the total cost. Companies can start with raw material cost and over the period of time, they can slowly build the full model on top of raw material cost.
How to build should cost model?
To build should cost model, organizations can use several tools such as excel or other analytical tools such as Decision Sigma. Many times we find excels are easy to use, but creating models using excel has many draw backs.
First, when models are built using excel, many times only one or two persons in the company can understand those excels. Also each person builds the model in his own way. Therefore, organizations fail to implement best practices and share lessons learned among different people to optimize models. Also when the person who manages models developed using excel leaves company or moves different role within same company, it becomes difficult for the next person to understand those excel and continue leveraging those should cost models.
Second, Models built using excels do not connect to any historical database nor it can integrate forecasted price of raw materials or sale volume that easily. Therefore, users fail to quantify historical missing opportunities or forecasted future opportunity. Sometimes users spend lot of time optimizing a should cost model for a particular raw material, but if that raw material is not forecasted to as high priority raw material then effort to optimize the models does not pay off. Lack of real time integration of the models with commodity price, historical purchase volume and forecasts sales volume contribute to wrong priorities and missed opportunities.
Finally, excels are easy to lose. When we lose excels, all the hard work put into building the model goes waste and organizations lose valuable intellectual asset built over period of time.
Analytic tools like DecisionSigma helps users build should cost models that connect to historical spend data, forecasted sales, bills of material and most importantly real time commodity market data. It allows entire depart to work on one system in more standardize manner, share best practice and develop multiple what-if should cost models.
